## Introduction

Running an Idris is not a solo act. It is one of the most demanding multi-crew experiences in Star Citizen, and the difference between a well-coordinated Idris wing and a disorganized one is immediately obvious the moment a contact appears. Done right, an Idris with a full crew and a healthy fighter wing is one of the most capable platforms in the 'verse. Done wrong, it's a very expensive wreck.

This guide covers how FSS runs Idris operations from the ground up: crew assignments, flight deck procedures, fighter wing management, combat employment, and communication protocol. Whether you're crewing for the first time or stepping into a command role, read this before you undock.

> **Variant note:** FSS operates both the **Idris-M** (military variant with the S10 spinal railgun) and the **Idris-P** (civilian variant without the spinal mount). Most of this guide applies to both. Where something is M-only, it's called out explicitly.

---

## The Ship

The Idris is a capital-class frigate. It is large, slow, and not built to dogfight. What it is built for is force projection — carrying a fighter wing, absorbing punishment, and delivering heavy firepower at range. On the FSS flight deck, we run a standard wing of **three F7 Hornets** and **one Gladiator**, which gives us a mix of air superiority and torpedo strike capability.

**What the Idris is:**
- A mobile command post and fighter carrier
- A serious anti-capital threat with the right crew (M variant)
- A deterrent that changes the calculus for any hostile org contesting an operation

**What the Idris is not:**
- Maneuverable. Don't try to turn-fight anything.
- Safe when undermanned. A half-crewed Idris is a liability.
- A solo ship. Never undock without a minimum crew assigned.

## Crew Assignments

The Idris has a lot of stations. At minimum you need enough people to cover the critical ones. Here's the breakdown:

### Command

| Role | Responsibility |
|---|---|
| **Captain (CO)** | Overall command. Engagement decisions, situational awareness, final call on all orders. Stays off flight controls unless necessary. |
| **Ship Pilot** | Flies the Idris. Positions the ship for optimal fighter support, weapons fire, and recovery heading. Communicates movement to the FDO. |
| **XO / Co-Pilot** | Supports the Captain. Manages nav, tracks contacts, relays orders when the Captain is heads-down. Takes command if CO is incapacitated. |

### Flight Deck

| Role | Responsibility |
|---|---|
| **Flight Deck Officer (FDO)** | Manages all launch and recovery operations. Authorizes every departure and return. Single point of authority for the flight deck. |
| **Fighter Pilots (x3)** | Fly the F7 Hornets. Execute CAP, escort, and intercept assignments. |
| **Gladiator Crew** | Two-seat torpedo bomber. Pilot and gunner/WSO. Employed for strike missions against capital ships or hardened targets. |

### Weapons

| Role | Responsibility |
|---|---|
| **Turret Crews** | Man the gun turrets (top, bottom, side). Coordinates with CO on engagement priority. |
| **Torpedo Operator** | Manages the ship's torpedo loadout. Works with CO to select targets and timing. |
| **Railgun Operator** *(Idris-M only)* | Operates the S10 spinal railgun. Requires clear sightlines — coordinate movement with the pilot to bring the gun to bear. |

### Support

| Role | Responsibility |
|---|---|
| **Engineering** | Monitors ship systems. Repairs damage during combat. Calls out critical system failures to the CO. |
| **Medical** | Handles crew casualties. Keeps respawn assets ready. |

**Minimum viable crew to undock:** Captain, Pilot, FDO, at least one fighter pilot. Everything else is a gap you'll feel in combat.

## Pre-Launch Checklist

Before anything leaves the deck, run this:

1. **Crew check** — confirm all stations are manned or acknowledged as unmanned gaps
2. **Fighter readiness** — all flight crew report ready to FDO
3. **Weapons status** — turrets crewed, torpedo tubes loaded, railgun charged (M only)
4. **Engineering baseline** — all systems green, any pre-existing damage noted
5. **Comms check** — all crew confirm on channel, callsigns assigned (see Communication Protocol)
6. **Route briefing** — CO gives the destination, anticipated threats, and rules of engagement

Do not skip the comms check. Finding out someone is on the wrong channel after a contact appears wastes time you won't have.

## Flight Deck Operations

The flight deck is the most operationally complex part of running an Idris. The FDO owns it. No ship launches or recovers without FDO authorization.

### Launch Procedure

1. **FDO calls the launch order** — e.g., *"Flight deck, launch on my mark. Alpha Flight, stand by."*
2. **Pilots spin up** on the deck and confirm ready
3. **FDO clears one ship at a time** unless a multi-ship launch is explicitly called — *"Alpha One, cleared for departure."*
4. **Pilot acknowledges and launches**
5. **FDO calls the next ship** once the previous is clear of the deck
6. **All fighters report feet wet** (clear of the Idris) to the FDO once airborne

Launching all four simultaneously is possible but chaotic. Use single launches unless time pressure demands otherwise.

### Recovery Procedure

Recovery is harder than launch. The Idris is moving, and an F7 approaching a moving deck has a much smaller margin for error than a conventional landing. This is where poor communication causes accidents.

**Recovery sequence:**

1. **Pilot calls inbound** — *"FDO, Alpha One is bingo [or: is RTB], requesting recovery."*
2. **FDO acknowledges and clears the deck** — only one ship recovers at a time
3. **Ship Pilot holds a straight, steady heading** during recovery — no turns, no speed changes unless absolutely necessary. Communicate any movement to FDO first.
4. **FDO clears recovery** — *"Alpha One, deck is clear, you are cleared to land."*
5. **Pilot approaches from directly aft**, matching Idris heading and speed, and sets down
6. **Pilot confirms deck** once landed — *"Alpha One, on deck."*
7. **FDO clears the next recovery**

**The number one cause of recovery accidents is the Idris changing heading without warning the incoming pilot.** Ship Pilot and FDO must stay in constant communication during any recovery operation.

**Emergency recovery:** If a fighter is critically damaged, FDO clears the deck immediately and all other recoveries hold. Damaged ship has priority.

## Combat Employment

### CAP — Combat Air Patrol

CAP is the default posture when no strike mission is active. The three F7s maintain a patrol envelope around the Idris, screening for threats and intercepting anything that gets close.

- Assign patrol sectors to each F7 (forward, port quarter, starboard quarter is a standard split for three)
- CAP fighters do not chase contacts deep — their job is to delay and report, not to solo enemy formations
- Any contact beyond 15 km should be reported to the CO immediately; engagement decision rests with the CO
- Rotate CAP assignments for long operations to keep pilots fresh

### Strike — Gladiator Deployment

The Gladiator is a torpedo bomber. It has no business in a knife fight. When a strike is called:

1. CO designates the target and authorizes strike
2. FDO coordinates a launch window clear of active recovery
3. **F7 escort is mandatory** — at least one Hornet flies with the Gladiator to suppress fighters while the torpedo run is set up
4. Gladiator crew selects approach vector and communicates to escort — *"Running in from bearing 045, pulling off right."*
5. Escort clears the approach lane of fighters
6. Gladiator makes its run, disengages, and RTBs with escort

Do not send the Gladiator alone. It will die before it reaches release point.

### Anti-Capital Engagement — Idris-M Railgun

The S10 spinal railgun is a devastating weapon against capital ships and hardened targets, but it requires setup. The railgun fires along the ship's centerline — the Ship Pilot has to align the entire ship with the target.

- CO designates target and calls for railgun solution
- Ship Pilot comes to the engagement heading and holds
- Railgun Operator confirms bearing and calls ready — *"Gun ready, on target."*
- CO authorizes fire
- After firing, Ship Pilot may need to re-align for follow-on shots — communicate this to turret crews so they know the ship is maneuvering

The Idris-P does not have the spinal mount. P variant crews rely on turrets and the fighter wing for anti-capital work — the Gladiator becomes even more important in that configuration.

### Defensive Operations

When the Idris itself is under attack:

- CAP fighters pull back to close perimeter — *"Alpha Flight, tighten CAP, we're taking contact."*
- Turret crews free to engage at will within CO's last ROE
- Engineering monitors for hull breach and shields
- FDO halts any pending recovery until the CO calls the threat cleared
- CO decides whether to fight, maneuver, or both

If fighters are recovering when contact hits, FDO calls a hold: *"All flights, hold recovery, hot deck."* Pilots in the pattern hold position and wait.

## Communication Protocol

Clear comms are what separate a functioning Idris crew from a disaster. Keep transmissions short, use callsigns, and don't talk over each other.

### Callsigns

| Station | Callsign |
|---|---|
| Captain | **Actual** |
| Ship Pilot | **Helm** |
| Flight Deck Officer | **Deck** |
| Fighter 1 | **Alpha One** |
| Fighter 2 | **Alpha Two** |
| Fighter 3 | **Alpha Three** |
| Gladiator | **Hammer** |
| Engineering | **Engineering** |

### Standard Calls

| Situation | What to say |
|---|---|
| Requesting launch | *"Deck, [callsign], ready for departure."* |
| Cleared to launch | *"[Callsign], cleared for departure."* |
| Requesting recovery | *"Deck, [callsign], RTB, requesting recovery."* |
| Cleared to land | *"[Callsign], deck is clear, cleared to land."* |
| On deck (landed) | *"[Callsign], on deck."* |
| Contact report | *"Actual, [callsign], contact [bearing/range], [type if known]."* |
| Engaging | *"[Callsign], engaging."* |
| Bingo fuel / ammo | *"Deck, [callsign], bingo [fuel/ordnance], RTB."* |
| Ship maneuvering | *"All stations, Helm, coming to [heading/maneuver]."* |

Keep the channel clean. If it's not operationally relevant, it doesn't go on comms.

## Emergency Procedures

### Pilot Down / Ejection
- FDO notes pilot's last position and reports to CO
- A recovery ship (or the Idris itself if close enough) retrieves the pilot
- CO determines whether to hold position or continue the operation

### Flight Deck Fire / Deck Accident
- FDO calls *"Deck emergency"* immediately
- All airborne flights hold and remain feet wet
- Engineering responds to the deck
- No recoveries until FDO calls deck clear

### Critical Damage to Idris
- Engineering calls out affected systems: *"Engineering, we've lost [system]."*
- CO assesses: fight through, disengage, or emergency RTB
- If RTB, FDO calls emergency recovery — fighters recover as fast as possible in priority order
- If the Idris is going down, CO calls abandon ship. Pilots stay airborne. Ground crew ejects and is recovered by surviving assets.

## A Note on Command

An Idris operation only works if the CO is actually commanding and everyone else is executing their role. The biggest failure mode is the Captain trying to fly the ship, manage the flight deck, operate the railgun, and talk on comms simultaneously. Pick your CO, trust them, and let them run it.

If you're new to Idris ops and want to crew before stepping into a command role, that is the right call. Learn the ship from the inside before you run it from the bridge.
